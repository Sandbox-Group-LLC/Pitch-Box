# Industry — Enterprise AI & Computing

Reusable market context for any pitch, content, or event strategy aimed at the enterprise
technology buyer — the world Intel plays in and Sandbox-XM produces events for. Covers the
data center, AI, silicon, cloud, and client-computing landscape as it affects how we design
experiences.

## The macro that matters

The semiconductor industry is in a once-in-a-generation cycle: AI infrastructure demand is
pulling compute investment forward by years. Every hyperscaler, enterprise IT shop, and OEM
is racing to deploy AI-capable silicon — and the company that owns the most seats at the
data center table (Intel) is simultaneously executing a turnaround under CEO Lip-Bu Tan
focused on engineering excellence, customer trust, and financial discipline.

For event design, this means: *the audience is not browsing — they are making high-stakes
infrastructure decisions on compressed timelines.* Our events must be **decision-useful**,
not inspirational. Demos over decks. Proof over promise.

## The buying committee

Enterprise technology purchase decisions in Intel's world involve:

- **CIO / CTO / VP Infrastructure** — economic buyer. Cares about TCO, vendor risk,
  platform longevity, and migration path.
- **VP Engineering / Platform Architect** — technical evaluator. Will only be moved by
  benchmarks, live demos, and architectural fit. Allergic to marketing.
- **Line-of-business leaders (AI/ML, Data, Product)** — increasingly the budget holders.
  Want to know: *can I run my model on this, faster and cheaper?*
- **Procurement / IT Operations** — influencer. Cares about standardization, support SLAs,
  ecosystem compatibility.
- **OEM / ISV partners** — co-sellers and ecosystem multipliers. Want roadmap visibility,
  early silicon access, co-marketing support.

## Competitive landscape (shapes the room)

| Competitor | Their claim | Intel's counter |
|---|---|---|
| **AMD** (EPYC, MI300X) | Data center share gains, competitive AI accelerators | Broader ecosystem, enterprise trust, foundry optionality |
| **NVIDIA** (GPU/AI dominance) | AI training and inference standard-bearer | Open alternatives (Gaudi), total platform cost, x86 ubiquity |
| **ARM ecosystem** (AWS Graviton, Ampere, Apple Silicon) | Power efficiency, cloud-native workloads | x86 software compatibility, enterprise migration cost |
| **TSMC / Samsung Foundry** | Manufacturing leadership (for now) | Intel Foundry Services — onshore manufacturing, geopolitical resilience |

For Sandbox-XM, this competitive context determines **what the keynote needs to prove** and
**what demos must be in the showcase**: it's always about answering the market's current
skepticism with evidence, not just messaging.

## Content angles that land

- **To the CIO:** *"Consolidate your AI and traditional workloads on one architecture —
  lower TCO, fewer vendors, one support contract."*
- **To the engineer:** *"Here's the benchmark. Here's the code. Run it yourself."*
  (Live demos > slides, always.)
- **To the partner:** *"Here's the roadmap. Here's your early access. Here's the
  co-marketing fund."*
- **To the press:** *"This is the inflection point."* (Product reveals, competitive firsts,
  executive access.)

## Event design implications

- **Demo-forward, not deck-forward.** Intel's technical audience trusts what they can touch.
  Showcase and breakout demos are the credibility engine.
- **Tiered programming.** Intel's audience spans press (wants scoops), partners (wants
  roadmaps), engineers (wants depth), executives (wants strategy). One keynote can't serve
  all — breakout tracks and curated experiences are essential.
- **Competitive tension is the subtext.** Every Intel event exists in the shadow of AMD,
  NVIDIA, and ARM gains. The experience must project confidence and evidence without
  appearing defensive.
- **Global footprint.** Intel's events span the US, Europe, and APAC. Regional production
  partners (e.g., I-Two in APAC for Computex) are part of the model. Cultural and
  operational adaptation is built into every program.
