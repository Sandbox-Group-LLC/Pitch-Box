# Industry — Government & Public Sector Technology

Reusable market context for events, content, and pitches targeting the government and
public sector technology buyer — the world Intel's Federal Summit lives in and where
Sandbox-XM has direct production experience.

## Why this industry matters to us

Intel's **Federal Summit** is a purpose-built, invite-only program for government, defense,
and intelligence community decision-makers. Sandbox-XM produced the 2026 Federal Summit
(DC, ~300 attendees, Westfields Marriott) — a 1.5-day event with keynotes, breakouts,
partner showcase, and meeting rooms. It is one of Intel's most strategically sensitive
programs: the guest list is curated, the content is tailored, and the operational security
bar is higher than any commercial event.

This is a growing vertical for Intel — and for Sandbox-XM.

## The macro

Government technology spending is in a structural shift driven by three forces:

1. **AI adoption pressure.** Executive orders on AI (the 2023 EO on Safe, Secure, and
   Trustworthy AI; subsequent agency implementation plans) are pushing federal agencies to
   deploy AI at scale — but with unique constraints around data sovereignty, explainability,
   and procurement.

2. **Cybersecurity & zero trust.** CISA's Zero Trust Maturity Model, NIST frameworks
   (800-53, CSF 2.0), and the ongoing threat landscape are making silicon-level security
   (Intel SGX, TDX, confidential computing) relevant at the hardware layer, not just the
   software layer.

3. **Supply chain & onshore manufacturing.** The CHIPS Act ($52.7B) is reshaping where
   semiconductors are made. Intel Foundry Services and Intel's US-based fabs (Arizona, Ohio,
   New Mexico) are directly relevant to the national security conversation. Government
   buyers care about *where* the chip is made, not just what it does.

## The buying committee (government)

Government technology procurement is structurally different from commercial:

- **Agency CIO / CTO** — sets the technology strategy and modernization roadmap. Influenced
  by policy mandates (FedRAMP, FISMA, zero trust EOs), not just market trends.
- **Program Manager (DoD / IC)** — owns a specific mission program and its budget. Cares
  about capability, timeline, and compliance — in that order.
- **CISO / Security Officer** — gatekeeper. Must validate that hardware meets security
  requirements (FIPS 140-3, Common Criteria, STIG compliance).
- **Contracting Officer / Acquisition Lead** — the process owner. Everything flows through
  FAR/DFAR compliance, contract vehicles (GSA, BPAs, IDIQs), and procurement rules.
- **Systems Integrator (SI)** — the implementation partner (Booz Allen, Leidos, SAIC,
  Accenture Federal, Deloitte). Often the real decision-maker on platform selection.
  Intel's SI relationships are critical.
- **Intel partner ecosystem** — OEMs (Dell Federal, HP Federal, Lenovo) and ISVs building
  government-certified solutions on Intel platforms.

## Operational differences from commercial events

Government events have distinct operational characteristics that Sandbox-XM has navigated:

- **Security clearance considerations.** Some attendees and topics may involve classified
  or sensitive information. Venue selection, room access, and content handling must account
  for this.
- **Photography and social media restrictions.** Some attendees (IC, DoD) cannot be
  photographed or named publicly. Event photography protocols must be pre-agreed.
- **Government ethics rules.** Federal employees face strict gift and hospitality rules
  (generally <$20 individual, <$50 annual from a single source). F&B, swag, and
  entertainment must comply.
- **Procurement language.** Content and messaging must avoid anything that could be
  construed as a sole-source recommendation. Positioning is always "platform" and
  "ecosystem," not "buy Intel."
- **Partner sensitivity.** Government SIs and OEMs are in the room as Intel's partners
  *and* as competitors to each other. Showcase layout and meeting room scheduling must be
  politically neutral.
- **Venue requirements.** Proximity to DC, secure meeting capability, government per-diem
  compliance for lodging rates.

## Content angles that land

- **To the agency CIO:** *"Intel's platform runs under every major government cloud —
  AWS GovCloud, Azure Government, Oracle — and it's manufactured on US soil."*
- **To the program manager:** *"Here's the deployment running in a comparable environment.
  Here's the timeline. Here's the SI who delivered it."*
- **To the CISO:** *"Confidential computing at the silicon layer — data protected even from
  the cloud provider. FIPS validated. STIG-ready."*
- **To the SI partner:** *"Here's the reference architecture. Here's the joint solution
  brief. Here's the customer you can call."*

## Event design implications

- **Curated > open.** The Federal Summit model is invite-only by design. The value is in
  the guest list — government decision-makers who rarely attend commercial conferences.
  Quality of attendance over quantity.
- **Showcase = proof.** Partner demos in the showcase must show working government use cases,
  not generic enterprise demos. Attendees are evaluators, not browsers.
- **Breakout depth matters.** Government technologists want 45–60 minute deep-dives with
  Q&A, not 20-minute product overviews. Session design should allow for real technical
  exchange.
- **Networking is the product.** The reception, the meals, and the hallway conversations are
  where relationships form. F&B and hospitality design is not an afterthought — it's part of
  the programming.
- **One-to-one meetings.** Dedicated meeting rooms for Intel + SI + government buyer
  conversations are essential. This is where pipeline moves.
